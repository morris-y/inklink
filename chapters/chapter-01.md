Once upon a time, on the banks of the river Hebrus, in a land that would one day be known as Thrace, there lived a shadow. A shadow should love who its owner loves and hate those who treat him badly. But loyalty is rarely that simple.

It had been a warm, windy night when the prince first turned to her with a smile and named her. He called her *skioula*, little shade, because he caught her peeping at him from behind a curtain. She hadn’t meant to stare, or more precisely to be caught staring. She’d heard the music faintly from some distant hall and walked towards it in a trance. 

The prince sat on a window ledge, silhouetted against the evening. His bare foot beat out a rhythm against the wall and his fingers danced lazily but expertly over the strings of his kithara.

*The moon cared little*

*He sang secrets to the sparrows*

*She overheard and stole them*

“I see you, skioula,” he said suddenly, and she jumped. “You’re good at hiding but I see you. You’re Hesypera, aren’t you? Your father is Brygias Archon?”

She nodded wordlessly, her heart beating in her ears. He swung off the ledge and walked quietly towards her. She knew he was six — only a year older than she was — but he seemed so much bigger.

“This is my lyre, my very favorite thing in the whole world. Do you want to hold it?” Without waiting for an answer he put the kithara in her hands, still warm from his palms. Her fingers stroked the silken wood. 

“Can you sing? What’s your favorite song?”

She sung it shyly and his hands magically plucked out the accompaniment. Later he told the music-master that the daughter of Brygias Archon wanted to learn how to play and that they’d take their lessons together. Her father roared with delight when he found out. 

“How can someone *this* tiny be *this* bold, Hesypera? Did you eat a bees’ nest? Is this belly filled with cleverness and honey?” her father roared, tickling her with his whiskers as she giggled and thrashed in his grasp. “It was well done. It is very good for girls to learn music, and better still to befriend the son of the king.”

Hesypera took up crouching like a mouse near whatever room the prince was in. People smiled when they saw the small girl sitting quietly in the corner, so totally absorbed in the small tasks her mother gave her. But more often they didn’t notice her at all, or forgot quickly that she was there. And yet always, always Hesypera listened, and in this way learned many things. 

She knew the king of the Eidonians always his son formally, with his name and title. The look in her beloved prince’s eyes as they spoke — half yearning to please, half hopeless — made her want to hate the king, but she could not. All children in the palace ran up to King Oeagrus fearlessly to be swung up on his knee and leave sticky with sweets. The great joy the king would have surely taken in fatherhood was tainted before the prince was one day old. 

Rumor said the queen’s first cry of pain had beckoned the goddess Eileithyia to her birthing-bed. The midwives had run out half out their minds with fear and awe. Rumor said that Calliope, the great muse herself, had appeared to announce the child and tell all how the king had been blessed: his heir was no less than the son of Apollo and would one day earn his place among the stars.

Oeagrus never spoke of it after, nor did anyone else in his presence. He was fair and wise but a man of iron, a fearsome warrior standing two heads above normal men. No one dared rankle his old wound. 

It was *supposed* to be an honor, of course, having the blood of a god in your lineage. Oeagrus built a new temple to Apollo and sacrificed lavishly every year; to do otherwise would invite the wrath of the gods upon his people. 

Everyone said the queen was a slender, graceful woman, with happy eyes and the gentlest voice you ever heard. She wasted away with fever soon after the birth. The king had once worshipped her every movement, but he did not visit her sickbed. He never held the child. 

If he’d spoken to her, maybe the queen would have wept and said that Apollo decieved her, came to her in the king’s own form, just like Zeus duped the mother of Heracles. Would he have believed it? A woman who could betray you could just as easily lie.  

Maybe he was too kind to disturb her healing with his great anger. Maybe he wished to punish her with his silence. Maybe the happier you are in love, the easier it is to suspect that it was only ever an illusion. Still, he had her grave cleaned every day and planted richly with roses - a gesture of piousness or regret, who knew? 

Of course, everyone watched the prince grow with bated breath. Would he be a warrior like Herakles, a great hunter like Meleager, monster-slayer like Perseus? What glory would he bring to the sons of the Hebrus? They waited for him to strangle snakes in his cradle, to cut down enemy soldiers like dandelions, to make spears and arrows fly as surely as the Fates.

The prince did none of those things. He was quicksilver where the king was iron, with wrists like a lark’s whistle and a body corded with lean muscle. He drilled in arms with the other boys, doing only as well as a mortal might.

Night after night King Oeagrus, thunder-browed, watched the slender figure of his heir roam the banks of the Hebrus and sing. All Thracians who heard him felt their cares slip away and thanked Apollo, god of healing and music, for sending them a prince who was both.

Hesypera trailed behind him, her own kithara limp in her hand, simply happy he existed, that she breathed the same air as him, that she alone was welcome to go wherever he went, that she would spend the rest of her life listening to his voice echo in the river.

At eleven years old Hesypera was already beginning to be beautiful. She had skin the color of soft wheat, thick hair obediently braided, and dark eyes that noticed everything. The priestesses said that she took deft care of the sacred owls, that she understood the ritual preparations and legends as well as someone twice her age. That she was clever, careful, modest, and diligent: all that a girl should be. Hesypera kept her eyes lowered demurely though her heart was singing at the praise. She knew it was wanton to show pleasure too openly.

She imagined herself at sixteen, robed in white, emerging newly anointed from the temple of Bendis. Framed by a delicate veil, her face (slimmer and paler and starrier-eyed than it was now) would glow with mystery and secret knowledge. People would part before her like they did now before her mother, and the king himself would consult her in matters of omen and sacrifice.

And the prince would look at her and not be able to stop looking, amazed that his little skioula was so beautiful and grave and admired. He would write aching songs about her in secret and finally he would ask her father for her hand.

The king, she knew, would be pleased with the match. Her father was no small lord but an archon, powerful and trusted by the king, and her mother was Hellenic by birth, high priestess of the temple of Bendis. It would be a union gods and men would smile on.

And perhaps Apollo himself would come and tell his son he had chosen well, and Calliope and her sister Muses would bless their wedding. She would bear him many children and at the end of their lives they would ascend to the stars together, like mortal Castor and his divine twin Pollux.

Upon Castor’s death the gods, touched by their devotion to each other, entwined them forever in the skies. It had happened before and there was no reason it would not be their fate too, especially because Bendis would surely vouch for her.

When she was old she would remember these first ten years as golden haze speckled with vivid details, faded by much touching. Her father’s metal drinking horn, burnished and slightly dented. Her mother’s scent of blood and flowers and flame. Her bare feet licking the stone floors of the palace as she ran giggling with the other children. Her dim silver needle slipping in and out of taut cloth. The coolness of the river Hebrus on hot, sticky afternoons, easily washing away her childish hurts and tiredness.

Good times pass in a blur; only in hindsight do you recognize them as happiness. It takes shock and misery and fear to pull life into sharp focus. One such thorn arrived on an ox cart shortly after her eleventh birthday.

She and prince were admiring the olive trees when a caravan came rattling up the road to the plains. His dark blue eyes met her brown ones - that was all it took for them to understand each other - and they hid themselves behind a rock to peep at the procession. It was less a caravan than a few shabby ox carts rumbling one after another. Two had covered tops, rigged like tents.

“A merchant,” the prince said with interest.

“Peddlers, more likely. Look how thin the oxen are,” murmured Hesypera.

“Odd. They don’t usually come around harvest.”

The caravan went rolling up the winding road, weaving between fields and houses, up directly to the walls surrounding the palace. This too was strange. A peddler would simply wind between the houses, hawk his wares, and be on his way. A merchant would stop his carts at the marketplace, set up stalls, and go up on foot to beg permission from the king to do business. What sort of person would dare wheel an ox-cart right to the king’s door?

“Whatever he peddles, he will get a worse bargain than he hoped,” the prince smiled. The guards, to Hesypera’s surprise, let the carts pass. They ran together straight up the hillside to a secret entrance in the walls only they knew.

Slipping, hot and dusty, through the cool stone hallways of the palace, they arrived in the main courtyard just in time to see the wheels grind to a halt. A curious crowd was gathering, servants and noblemen and soldiers alike.

The oxen-driver was a spindly man, scurrying around nervously, helping the people in the cart disembark. First was a woman, round-shouldered and plump, with a distinctly flyaway, distracted air about her that cheapened the richness of her attire. Her apple-cheeked face was set in a frown; she spoke sharply to someone in the cart and harangued the servants about unloading their bags carefully.

First was a woman, round-shouldered and plump, with a distinctly flyaway air about her that cheapened the richness of her attire. Her apple-cheeked face was twisted in a frown; she snapped at someone in the cart and complained loudly that no one had the sense to unload a poor woman’s possessions carefully these days.

Hesypera and the prince exchanged a grin. Having had her fill of haranguing the servants, the woman stretched as unselfconsciously as any peasant and looked around approvingly, a smile suddenly coming over her like sunshine. It was a marvel, Hesypera thought, what a difference a smile could make: she’d seemed a detestable crank only seconds ago and now looked jolly and kindhearted and twenty years younger.

“You!” she said, pouncing on an unsuspecting guard. “Tell your king I summon him here straightaway.”

The guard gaped.

“Lady,” the guard stuttered, looking to his captain for guidance. “If you request an audience with the king…”

“I do *not* request an audience!” the woman said loudly and stubbornly. “I want the king *here*, in the next five minutes, or he will suffer my displeasure. Can you bring him or not?”

The ox-driver turned white. “Lady, please, consider…” he squeaked.

“Lady, I…” said the guard helplessly.

“*Why are you still standing here*? Is the king sick? Is he dead? Bring him out!”

The guard, apparently lost for words, slowly turned and walked into the hall. The woman seemed totally at ease, oblivious to the alarmed silence that had descended over everyone watching. 

Out of nowhere she spun around and demanded something of the ox-driver. The man seemed unwilling, but the woman was insistent, and soon he handed over…his cloak? She pulled it on and drew the hood low over her face. A very torn, dusty old thing it was, and made her even shabbier than the servants.

“She’s mad,” said Hesypera blankly.

“WHO DARES COMMAND KING OEGRUS IN HIS OWN HALL?” thundered the king, striding outside, his voice echoing in the stone pavilion. The greatest warriors of the Edonians were all around him, and every man had his hand on his sword. Hesypera was briefly reminded of Zeus levying his thunderbolt; the king’s long beard fairly crackled with lightning. His eyes flashed as they rove from man to man, all of whom looked at the floor. 

Then a girl, who’d slipped out of the cart without Hesypera noticing, pointed in solemn silence at the oxen driver. The king turned to him in a blaze of rage, and the man collapsed to his knees in hysterics and flattened his face on the floor.

“Mercy! Mercy! Mercy!”

The woman started laughing, a loud, ringing laugh, and the king looked up, confused.

“Well, Oegrus, you rascal. Is this how a man greets his elders and betters?”

She threw the hood off her face, and the king gave a start and dropped his sword. With a great peal of laughter he strode over and lifted her bodily off the floor in a great bear-hug.

“Oelyxena! My dearest girl, we did not expect you in the slightest! My older sister, married almost thirteen years ago to a Dacian lord,” he announced loudly, still beaming, for the benefit of the audience. “Unload their things, make them comfortable, nothing but the best.” The crowd broke, many laughing in relief; a few jumped to make arrangements.

The prince and Hesypera ran towards them, ready to be introduced to this fascinating new personage. The ox-driver rose up to sitting, still hyperventilating; Hesypera noted the man had wet himself. Oegrus took him by the arms and lifted him to standing, slapping him jovially on the back.

“There, there, my fine man. No harm done. Go have some wine, eh?” The ox-driver made an incoherent noise of thanks and tottered off, apparently incapable of speech.

“You keep a valiant personal guard, sister,” the king said, eyes twinkling. The girl who had pointed was looking after the driver and smirking, and his eyes fell on her. 

“And who is this wicked maiden who dares play pranks on a king?”

“My one child, your niece.” Oelyxena said fondly, drawing the girl to her side. The girl pulled away from her mother’s grasp impatiently and dipped into a graceful curtsy.

“Greetings, oh great King Oegrus. I beg forgiveness for the trick and thank you for your hospitality.”

Oegrus bent down and fixed a very stern eye on her. “Hmph! Girls should be respectful and obedient. Your hair needs combing, child.” It did, at that. It was a long waterfall of tangles, held only half in place with a bone pin that was presently askew. Hesypera wondered that her mother did not say anything. She could already see the priestesses clucking with disapproval and illustrating, with a fable, how careless girls came to no good.

The girl lifted her face fearlessly.

“No more than your beard does, uncle.”

King Oegrus threw his head back in laughter and tweaked her cheek.

“A copy of your mother, I see! Careful, child, or you will turn out just as badly when you are grown. Oh, you poor grandmother would weep to see you!” He noticed the prince standing beside him.

“Oelyxena, here is the Prince Orpheus. Orpheus, greet your aunt.” Orpheus bowed his head regally.

“Greetings, Lady Oelyxena.” He was only twelve, but already they were of a height.

“Call me Aunt. How handsome and strong he is! I am very glad to meet you, my dear son.” She cupped his face in her hand and kissed him warmly on the cheek. The prince went very still, his cheeks and ears reddening. As though in a trance he turned to watch her go. Hesypera stared too, bemused at fierce King Oegrus chatting gaily as a boy with the sister she never knew he had.

The girl tapped the prince on the shoulder.

“Orpheus? It is good to meet you. I am Eurydice.”
