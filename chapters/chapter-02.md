The Lady Olyxena was like no noblewoman Hesypeira had ever met. Her bearing was utterly undignified and her ways were loud and careless. But she was easy to love: full of laughter and jokes, lavish with kisses and praise and eager to be of service. She doted on Hesypeira and Orpheus, cooking them special treats and regaling them with stories of the mischievous children their parents had been. Yes, everyone, including Hesypeira, loved Olyxena. The older servants remembered her as a girl and she was the same blood as their own king.

Eurydice was different. There was something unsettling about Eurydice.

They whispered that her father was a wood-god, a servant of Pan, that there was bewitchment in her. Hesypeira sometimes wondered if she was even truly Lady Olyxena’s child; she looked and moved nothing like her mother. An odd scent hung about her wherever she went, a smell like crushed stems and darkened pools that Hesypeira instinctively disliked.

She was glad that there was no talk of Eurydice joining the cult of Bendis. The priestesses criticized her constantly. Eurydice woke up as early or late as she pleased. She disappeared for entire days to wander the woods like a peasant. She climbed trees and played with the goatherds and her hair was always a mess. She even put grubs in her pockets to sneak as treats to the sacred owls, whose feeding was a ritual assigned to Hespeirya - an honor she had won with many years of diligent effort.

It annoyed Hesypeira that the owls warbled when they saw Eurydice and rubbed their heads against her hands. Even the priestesses seemed to like her — they’d scold her endlessly for her heedlessness but braid flowers in her hair. Hesypeira sat at her loom with perfect dignity watching the tips of their fingers slip in and out of her curls, hearing them laugh at her insolence.

The worst was Orpheus, who instantly decided that Eurydice was their friend. Wherever they went, he invited her. He showed her all their secret spots and hiding places. He offered her everything from his woolen tunic if she was cold to his kithara if she wanted to practice. Hesypeira told herself it was because the prince was kind and gentle. Eurydice was his cousin, if not exactly by blood, and if rumors were true she and her mother had fled to King Oeagrus for refuge.

One day she could take it no longer. They were sitting together on the stone steps outside the palace enjoying the fickle winter sun, the prince tuning his kithara, Hesyperia mending a hole in her father’s robes.

“Shall we take a boat out onto the river tonight?” Hesypeira suggested.

“Would you like to go?” asked the prince, turning to her with a gentle smile.

“Yes,” she said, her heart catching at his gaze.

“Then we will. I’ll tell the servants to prepare the boats and the cooks to pack us honey cakes, and figs, and some olives, and fresh water and wine….”

Hesyperia loved how courteous and considerate Orpheus was. He went out of his way to fulfill her little wishes, made her feel like a princess herself.

“…and I’ll find Eurydice and tell her.” All of a sudden her throat soured.

“Why?” she said, trying to keep her voice even.

“Why not? She’d like it. The moon will be beautiful on the river and she always says how much nicer moonlight is than sunlight.”

“We always do things with her.”

“Do you not like Eurydice?” Orpheus asked, his brow furrowing. At the trouble in his voice Hesypeira softened.

“No, of course I do. Only we used to spend so much time together, just you and me, and now we never do.”

“We’re together right now,” Orpheus laughed.

“Yes, but I mean…” What *did* she mean? She didn’t want to sit with him in the courtyard, surrounded by servants and the occasional errant hen. She wanted him in the shadows, under the stars, where there was no one to watch them and nowhere to look but his eyes. She wanted the magic between them, the magic she felt so strongly when they were alone, to build up until words of passion poured out of his mouth like music. She wanted to brush her lips against his perfect shoulder, to run her fingers through his silken gold hair, to tie them together…somehow…forever.

The words choked her, so instead she straightened her back, smoothed her hair, smiled demurely, and said in her gentlest, noblest voice, “I’d love it if she came.”

They pushed off in the evening, an evening that sunk quickly into a ripe, almost feverish night. The moon hung heavy in the sky and the homelike Hebrus became a different river entirely, dark and seemingly endless.

Hesypeira sat delicately on the cushion Orpheus picked out for her, her feet crossed primly and her hands busy with needlework. The boat felt oddly off balance, or maybe it was the river itself, Hesyperia thought bitterly, straining, like everything else, towards Eurydice.

She stood against the edge of the boat, the moonlight glinting off her waterfall of black ringlets. A single torch threw her saffron dress and slender arms, bare against the wind, into sharp relief. She burned against the darkness, her face tipped back to drink up the moon.

Hesypeira could not stop looking at her - the strange hungry look in her eyes, the line of her jaw, how her body curved above the belt of her tunic. Orpheus’s music rose and fell effortlessly to match the oars, but there was something wilder than usual in it tonight. Hesypeira felt a pull below her stomach, a restless urge she didn’t recognize, to pace or dance or pick a fight.

She laid down her needlework and, almost without knowing what she was doing, walked over to join Eurydice. The scent of the other girl hit her, mixing strangely with the familiar smell of the river.

“How have you been liking your time here?” she asked, mostly to break the silence.

“Everyone is very kind, but the women are dull compared to my father’s court.” Hesyperia was taken aback at her rudeness.

“On behalf of all ignorant Edonian women, I apologize for disappointing you,” she said cuttingly.

Eurydice laughed and looked at Hesypeira, her eyes suddenly sparkling. “I don’t mean you. I think you’re beautiful and very clever and I want to be friends with you more than anything.”

“Oh.” Hesypeira said. Her stomach churned with annoyance and an unwelcome warmth.

“But you don’t like me.” Eurydice said, no trace of resentment in her voice. “Why?”

Hesypeira could not help it; her eyes flickered to Orpheus. He sat on the far edge of the boat, half shrouded in darkness. His fingers still moved skillfully over the strings of the kithara, but his mind was clearly elsewhere. He was staring at her with an intensity she had never seen. And suddenly the hard knot in her chest loosened and she was ready to befriend anybody.

“You’re ungrateful,” she said, secretly thrilling at her bluntness. “You are surrounded by people who wish you well and want to teach you to become a young lady your family would be proud of, but you spend your time running around the forest. And you eat and drink and live at the palace but you don’t help with the chores.”

“All right. What should I do?”

“Take lessons like we other girls do. Learn history and mu…weaving with us. Help prepare meals and fetch water and weave and mend. And stop feeding my owls!”

Eurydice tilted her head and considered this. “I did not see that it was ungrateful. I never did these things in my father’s court.”

Hesypeira hesitated and asked, “What was it like at your father’s court?”

“You mean was my father really a cruel wood god like they say?” Eurydice said calmly. Hesypeira blushed.

“Two years after my parents were married, there was a terrible drought. My mother was young and high spirited and outspoken, and she’d long irritated an certain old woman who used to having everything her way before the new queen. The old woman proclaimed that a soothsayer had revealed to her that my mother was the cause of the drought, that she had committed a secret sin against men and the gods.”

Dimly Hesypeira was aware that Orpheus had stopped playing. Eurydice’s voice was hard.

“My father was not a bad man, but I think he was weak. The old woman brought all her allies and the priests together, and they said to offer my mother to the old wood gods as a test of her purity, or maybe a sacrifice. I don’t know.”

Silence, broken only by the oars.

“She doesn’t remember much of it. They gave her something in her wine and took her to a mountain. She was there for a few days but reappeared, alive and unhurt, at the palace gates in the middle of a storm. They said the rain had returned as a reward for our piety and a confirmation of her virtue. I was born a few months later.”

Hesypeira found her voice again.

“So are you….”

“The daughter of a wood god? I don’t think so.” She stared across the river moodily before adding, “I’m a girl like any other.” Hesypeira delicately held back a snort.

“I could believe it,” Orpheus said quietly, and resumed playing, a harmony filled with darkness and grief.

“What happened to your father? The man your mother married I mean?”

“My father.” Eurydice said firmly. “He loved me. And I loved him. He got sick. And the old woman said my mother and I had done it, that we had poisoned him.”

“And they believed it?”

“After he died, we didn’t stay around to find out. They believed her once.”

Hesypeira thought of the Lady Oelyxena, laughing and good natured, the way the youth seemed sapped from her face even though she wasn’t that old. Hesypeira, with her childhood habit of moving quietly in the dark, had seen the sudden tears Olyxena wept sometimes, the weariness of her movements when she thought she was alone.

*If I have a gift*, she thought, *it’s seeing what people are like when no one is watching.* Her heart wrung with pity. *I’ll be nice to Eurydice, even if she’s strange, for Oelyxena’s sake.*
